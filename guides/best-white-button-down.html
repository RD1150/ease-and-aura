How to Choose the Best White Button-Down

The shirt that reads as effort — and the one whose flaws are hardest to hide.

A white button-down is the most quietly useful thing in a capsule. It goes under a sweater, over a tank, with jeans on Saturday and with trousers on Monday, and it makes almost any outfit look like a decision rather than an accident.

It's also the least forgiving garment you will ever buy, for one reason: white hides nothing. A thin fabric goes see-through. A cheap button reads as cheap from four feet away. The doubled fabric of the placket and the interfacing inside the collar show through as brighter shapes. A navy shirt forgives all of this. A white one broadcasts it. So the bar is higher — and the good news is that everything that separates a good white shirt from a bad one is visible before you buy, if you know the four things to look at.

1. The weave tells you what shirt you're actually buying

Nearly every white shirt is cotton, so "100% cotton" tells you far less than people think. The weave is what determines how the shirt looks, how it behaves, and whether you can see through it — and it's almost always stated on the product page.

Poplin

A tight, flat, plain weave. It's smooth and crisp, it presses to a sharp finish, and it's what most people picture when they picture a white shirt. It's the most formal of the three and the most flattering under a blazer. The catch: poplin is lightweight, and in white it's the weave most likely to be sheer.

Oxford

A basketweave — thicker yarns crossed in pairs — which gives it a faint visible texture and noticeably more heft. Oxford is the casual one: softer, sturdier, harder to wear under tailoring, and the most forgiving choice by a distance. It's more opaque, more durable, and it hides wrinkles better. If your shirt is going to live with jeans, this is the smarter weave.

Twill

A diagonal weave with a subtle sheen and real drape. It's softer than poplin, resists wrinkling better than either of the others, and skims the body rather than standing away from it. It reads slightly less crisp and slightly more expensive.

The shortcut: buying one white shirt for tailoring and photographs? Poplin — and check it for sheerness. Buying one white shirt to actually live in? Oxford. It is the more useful shirt and the harder one to ruin.

2. Opacity is the white shirt's specific failure

This is the one that sends shirts back. A white shirt can be flawlessly made and still be unwearable because you can read the outline of everything underneath it.

Two numbers predict it. The first is fabric weight — if it's listed, anything around 120 gsm or below in poplin is likely to be sheer, and 140 gsm and up is generally safe. The second is counterintuitive: a very high yarn count can work against you. Yarn counts like 100s or 120s mean finer threads, which is a genuine marker of luxury and produces a smoother, silkier fabric — but finer threads also mean a thinner, more transparent, more delicate shirt. On a white shirt specifically, the fanciest number on the page is not the one you want to maximize.

If the weight isn't listed, the reviews will tell you — "sheer," "had to wear a cami," and "you can see my bra" are the most reliable free information on the internet. And when the shirt arrives, hold it up to a window before you cut the tags: if you can make out your hand clearly through a single layer, it will be worse under office lighting than it is in your bedroom.

One more thing to look for in the photos: on a too-thin shirt, the placket and the collar look brighter than the rest of the shirt, because those panels are two or three layers thick over stiff interfacing. If you can see those shapes in the product photo, you'll see them on your body.

3. The details that separate a $40 shirt from a $200 one

Shirts are where small construction choices are unusually visible, and most of them are checkable in a product photo.

The buttons. The single fastest tell. Real mother-of-pearl has depth — it shifts in the light, and each one is slightly different because it was cut from a shell. Cheap resin buttons are flat, uniform, faintly plasticky, and thin enough to crack in a dryer. Zoom in on the photo. On a white shirt, where the buttons are one of only two details you can see, this matters more than it sounds.

The collar. Ask whether the interfacing is fused or sewn. Fused interfacing is glued on — it's cheaper, and after enough washes the glue fails in patches and the collar bubbles and ripples permanently. A sewn-in or floating collar costs more and keeps a soft, live roll for the life of the shirt. Related: removable collar stays (usually metal, in a slot on the underside) are a good sign; permanently sewn-in plastic stays are the cheap version and they warp in the wash.

The gauntlet button. That's the small extra button partway up the sleeve placket, above the cuff. It closes the slit that otherwise gapes open when you bend your arm. It costs a manufacturer almost nothing and it's routinely omitted, which makes its presence a decent proxy for whether anyone was paying attention.

The seams. Turn the shirt inside out, or find the photo that shows the inside. A good shirt has flat-felled or French seams — the raw edge is folded and enclosed, so there's nothing to fray and the seam lies flat against you. An overlocked seam with a fuzzy raw edge serged over is the fast, cheap version, and it's the first thing to unravel.

The split yoke. Look at the panel across the upper back. If it's two pieces joined by a center seam rather than one, that's a split yoke: it lets the maker angle the grain to each shoulder for a better fit, and it's a small piece of evidence that the shirt was patterned by someone who cared.

4. The gape at the bust is a pattern problem, not a size problem

If you have a bust, you already know the failure: the shirt fits everywhere else and pulls into a diamond-shaped gap at the button across the chest. Almost everyone responds by sizing up, and then wears a shirt that's tented everywhere else to solve one inch in one place.

The real cause is that most button-downs are patterned with the buttons evenly spaced from a straight line, without a button placed at the fullest point of the bust. Three things actually work:

Buy for the bust and have the waist taken in — a shirt is one of the cheapest, most reliable things a tailor can alter, usually for less than the shirt cost. Or buy a deliberately relaxed or boyfriend cut, which has enough ease through the chest that there's nothing to pull against; this is the easy answer and the reason that silhouette has stayed in style. Or look for brands that place a button at the apex and cut a proper bust dart — increasingly common, and worth searching for by name.

What about stretch? A few percent elastane does reduce gaping, and it's a legitimate reason to choose it. Be clear-eyed about the trade: elastane pills, and it breaks down with heat and time, so a stretch shirt has a shorter life and will eventually go slack and wavy along the placket in a way 100% cotton never does. If you buy stretch, wash it cool and keep it out of the dryer, and you'll get most of the life back.

The rest of the fit is the ordinary checklist: the shoulder seam should land at the corner of your shoulder (it's the one seam a tailor can't really fix), the cuff should sit at the base of your thumb, and the body should be long enough to stay tucked when you raise your arms — lift them in the fitting room and see what escapes.

How to keep it white

White shirts rarely wear out. They go dingy, and then they get retired — which means care isn't a footnote here, it's most of how long the shirt lasts.

The counterintuitive part: chlorine bleach is what's making your white shirts yellow. It reacts with body oils and protein residue left in the fibers and sets a yellow cast that gets worse each time, and it weakens the cotton along the way. Use oxygen bleach instead — the powdered kind you dissolve in warm water — which brightens without the yellowing.

The other culprit is greying from buildup: too much detergent and too full a machine mean the residue never fully rinses out, and it dulls white cotton a shade at a time. Use less detergent than the cap suggests, and give the load room to move. Pre-treat the collar and cuffs before they look dirty rather than after, since that grime is body oil and it sets with every hot cycle.

And the free one: dry it in the sun. Sunlight genuinely bleaches white cotton — it's the one laundry folk remedy that's straightforwardly true, it costs nothing, and it's gentler than anything you can buy.
