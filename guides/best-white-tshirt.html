How to Choose the Best White T-Shirt

The most-recommended piece in fashion, and the one people most often get wrong.

Every capsule wardrobe list opens with "a good white tee," and then declines to explain what makes one good. So people buy a three-pack, discover it's see-through in daylight, wash it twice, watch it go limp and grey, and quietly conclude they're bad at this.

They're not. Most white t-shirts are genuinely bad. They fail in three predictable ways — opacity, fabric, and fit — and once you know what to check, you can rule out the duds without trying anything on.

1. Opacity is the first test, and most tees fail it

White is the least forgiving color there is. A tee that looks crisp folded on a shelf can turn sheer the moment you step outside, and no amount of styling fixes it.

The cause is almost always weight, measured in GSM (grams per square meter). It's rarely on the product page, but you can infer it. Under about 140 GSM, a white tee will read as thin and likely show whatever is underneath. Around 160–200 GSM is the range that stays opaque, holds a clean line, and still drapes rather than standing up like cardboard. Above 220 you're into heavyweight, boxy territory — great if that's the look, wrong if you want it under a blazer.

The thirty-second test: hold the shirt up to a window with your hand behind it. If you can make out your fingers clearly, it will be see-through on a sunny day. Also check the shoulder seam area, where a lot of thin tees give out first.

2. Fabric decides whether it survives the wash

Two tees can both say "100% cotton" and behave completely differently, because the cotton itself isn't the same.

Worth your money

Long-staple cotton — sold under names like Pima or Supima — uses longer fibers, which means a smoother surface, less pilling, and far less of that fuzzy, worn-out look after a few months. Combed cotton has had the short fibers mechanically removed, which does much the same job. Either is a real signal that the maker cared.

Be cautious

Plain "100% cotton" with no further description is usually short-staple, and it's the reason cheap tees go from crisp to sad so quickly. A small amount of elastane (2–5%) helps a fitted tee hold its shape, but more than that and it'll start to look shiny and cling in ways you didn't intend. Anything mostly polyester will hold its shape but never quite look expensive.

One more thing worth knowing: pre-shrunk or "garment-washed" on the label means the shrinking already happened at the factory rather than in your machine. Without it, expect to lose meaningful length the first wash — which is how a perfect tee becomes a crop top.

3. Fit is three measurements, not one size

Sizing labels are close to meaningless across brands. What actually determines whether a tee looks intentional comes down to three places.

The shoulder seam should land at the edge of your shoulder bone, or clearly past it if the cut is deliberately relaxed. What looks wrong is the in-between — half an inch off, which reads as ill-fitting rather than oversized. The sleeve should end at roughly the mid-bicep; sleeves that stop right at the widest part of the arm are the least flattering length on almost everyone. And the hem should hit at your hip bone or just below, so it tucks cleanly and doesn't ride up when you raise your arms.

4. The neckline changes what the shirt is for

A crewneck is the most classic and the most versatile, but it needs a neckline that sits flat — if the ribbing is thin or stretched, it'll gape and the whole shirt reads cheap. Check that the collar ribbing is dense and springs back. A v-neck elongates the neck and is more forgiving if a crewneck makes you feel closed-in. A scoop or boat neck reads softer and works better under a blazer where a crewneck can look cluttered against the lapel.

If you're buying one white tee, buy the crewneck. It does the most jobs.

What we'd buy, at three budgets

Rather than name a single "best" tee — stock changes constantly, and fit is personal — here's where we'd look at each tier, and what to hold out for.

Foundation Roughly $15–35

How to keep it white

White tees don't usually go grey from dirt — they go grey from detergent residue and from being washed with everything else. Wash whites together, in cold water, and use less detergent than you think you need. Skip the fabric softener entirely; it coats the fibers and dulls them. And air-dry if you can, because the dryer is what breaks down the collar and shrinks the length.

Done that way, a good tee stays crisp for years instead of months — which is the whole argument for buying a good one.
